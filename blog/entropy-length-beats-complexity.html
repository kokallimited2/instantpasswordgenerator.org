Entropy Explained: Why Password Length Beats Complexity Rules

10 Apr 2026·6 min·Alex Chen, IT Security Specialist

Most password policies enforce complexity rules. NIST SP 800-63B explicitly discourages them — and the mathematics explains why.

The Entropy Formula

Password entropy: H = L × log₂(N) where H = entropy in bits, L = length, N = character pool size.

A 16-character all-lowercase password (75 bits) is harder to brute-force than an 8-character mixed password (52 bits), even though it fails most legacy complexity rules.

Why Complexity Rules Backfire

Composition rules constrain users into predictable patterns: capital at the start, number and symbol at the end, minimum required change each rotation cycle. Targeted wordlists exploit these patterns and crack "complex" passwords faster than random lowercase equivalents of the same length.

What NIST Recommends Instead

  • Length minimum: 15 characters (NIST SP 800-63B 2025 final)
  • Accept up to: 64 characters at minimum
  • No composition rules
  • Breach corpus check at creation and reset
  • No rotation without evidence of compromise
Bulk generation takeaway: At 20 characters with all character classes the Instant Password Generator produces 131 bits of entropy — computationally infeasible against any realistic attack. Length is your primary security lever, not complexity rules.
